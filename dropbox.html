Dropbox UX Writing

UX Writing Challenge • 1 Week 2020

Overview

This project is the first part of the Dropbox's UX writing challenge. This was my first attempt at a UX writing task. This project outlines my strengths and interest in exploring how language can be communicated.

Final Deliverable

Communication Goals

My task was to write an email that's sent to users when they're about to run out of space on Dropbox Mocks, a web app that lets users design websites and apps. Here's the scope of the challenge:

  • Assume users are somewhat technical. They are probably designers who have been using Mocks for a while.
  • Provide the paid plan but don't push the users too hard. Here, the priority is to make sure they can keep using the product.
  • Sound like Dropbox: straightforward, helpful, and human.
  • Write to a 6th-grade or so reading level
Annotate improvements

First Iteration

Feedback
  • Place content above the fold. Consider users viewing this email on their phones (smaller screens)
  • Add delight when it is right. Take langugage translation into consideration.
  • Add 'value' to solutions. Why are these solutions valuable?

Breaking Down the Process

Part A.
Improvements

I decided to remove the image and move the text to the top. While visual relief is always valuable to give the readers a break, I realized that noteworthy content should appear above the fold. Especially when users have a high chance of viewing the email on their phones, with even less screen real estate avaiable, moving the content to the top was a priority.

Out of the 4 options I explored, I chose the most straightforward and scannable clause.

Part B.
Improvements

I wanted to surface the most scannable information to the top but also clearly communicate how much storage is left. I decided to choose a percentage and also include a GB conversion to give the readers flexibility in metrics.

I removed the word 'sadly' from the transition because translation can go wrong in different languages. I learned to be emotionally aware by removing text that may feel like I am presuming what another person is feeling.

Part C.
Improvements

I wanted to highlight the problem and the solutions on this email. In order to focus on the visual hierarchy, I used increased weight on action words on each bullet and used a list to make the solutions easily scannable.

I learned that it is important to shorten your text, but it is still important to be detailed enough. In order to communicate 'why' people should follow these actions, I thought about the value of each action I was recommending.

Evaluate and Verify

For my final checkup, I went to verify my reading level on different platforms.

Final Deliverable

Moving Forward

I am grateful that I had the opportunity to express and explore my interest in UX writing despite not having any past experiences in this field. I was able to explore the role words play in shaping people's end-to-end experiences.
